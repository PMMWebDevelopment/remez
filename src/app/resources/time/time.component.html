Dates and Time in Hebrew

Dates

There are two calendars in use in Israel, the traditional Jewish calendar, which is based on lunar months, and the Gregorian calendar. The Jewish calendar shares much of its background and its construction with its Islamic counterpart, and both systems are adhered to by traditionalists in either faith. The Jewish calendar is also the primary official one in use in Israel, partly because it governs when Jewish holidays occur. There are some purposes, however, for which the Gregorian calendar is used, e.g. for determining the start and end of Israeli Summer Time.

The Jewish calendar is a little more complicated than the Gregorian one in that the resulting year is around eleven days shorter than the true solar year. Therefore, in seven out of every 19 years, the last month of the Jewish year, אדר Adar, is repeated. When this occurs, the first "edition" of the month is considered to be the "leap" element, so that any high days normally occurring in Adar do so in the second "edition". This most usually affects the festival of פורים Purim, which falls on the 14th Adar.

Confusingly, while the ecclesiastical Jewish year begins with ניסן Nisan, the civic year begins with תשרי Tishri, so that ראש משנה Rosh ha-Shanah, the Jewish new year, occurs on 1st Tishri and therefore usually in the Gregorian month of September.

Whatever system is used, expressing a calendar date is done simply by saying the "Xth day of" whatever month.

The number of the civic Jewish year is calculated using תשרי Tishri as the first month, and the new Jewish year which starts on 1st Tishri is numbered by taking the Gregorian year number and adding 3,761, so that the Jewish year 5779 begins in September 2018. Bear in minde, though, that the ecclesiastical year with that number already began six months before that. This can lead to some confusion during that period between the two systems, but it is always the month of אדר Adar which is repeated.

Determining whether a year is leap is done by dividing the number of the year in the Jewish calendar by 19 and finding the remainder. If the remainder is 0, 3, 6, 8, 11, 14 or 17, the year is leap. The current 19-year-cycle began on 9th April 2016 (1st Nisan 5776), so that, going forward, the years 5779 (2018-19), 5782 (2021-22), 5784 (2023-24), 5787 (2026-27), 5790 (2029-30) and 5793 (2032-33) will be leap. The next cycle will begin on 10th April 2035.

Traditionally, Jews have considered each day to begin at sunset. Celebrations of Jewish high days and holidays often begin in the evening (Friday in the case of the sabbath). However, for official purposes, Israel operates on a midnight-to-midnight basis along with the rest of the world. Israel is either two or three hours ahead of Greenwich Mean Time. As with most other countries outside the tropics, Israel operates daylight saving time. Clocks go forward one hour in Israel at 2am local time on the Friday before the last Sunday in March, and go back one hour at 2am local time on the last Sunday in October. The Gregorian calendar is used to determine these dates (see the section on the right).
